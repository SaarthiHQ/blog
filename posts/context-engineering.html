Context Engineering Is Not Context Stuffing

By Ramanan Sivasubramanian · March 2026

In my previous post, I argued that context engineering is reshaping on-call. A few people asked the obvious question: why can't you just give the LLM all the logs and let it figure it out?

Because that doesn't work. And the research explains exactly why.

"Can Accept" Is Not "Can Use"

We talk about models with 100K, 200K, even 1M token context windows as if capacity equals capability. It doesn't. Three papers make this uncomfortably clear.

Lost in the Middle

Liu et al. · Stanford · 2023

LLMs exhibit a U-shaped attention curve — strong at the beginning and end of the context, significant degradation in the middle. With 20+ documents, performance dropped below the no-context baseline. More data made the model less accurate than giving it nothing.

Needle in the Haystack

Nelson et al. · IBM Research · 2024

Even simple fact retrieval — finding a single planted sentence — breaks down as context length increases. Longer window, more noise, worse results.

Prompt Repetition

Leviathan et al. · Google Research · 2025

The order of information materially changes output quality. Repeating the query after the context improved accuracy in 47/70 tests with 0 regressions. Causal models process left-to-right and can't attend backwards.

What Does 3,000 Tokens Actually Look Like?

LLM reasoning degrades around 3,000 tokens. Here's how much that is:

~2,000 words
3K tokens
~50-60 log lines
3K tokens
1 min of production logs
~18K tokens
15 min incident window
~270K tokens

Every token you send needs to earn its place.

What This Means in Practice

These aren't academic curiosities. If you're building any AI system that consumes real-world data — incident response, healthcare, legal, customer support — the implications are direct.

Filter before you prompt

Don't send the model everything. Filter by time window, by service, by severity. Score by relevance. 500 filtered lines beat 5,000 raw lines every time.

Order matters

Put high-signal information at the beginning and end. The middle is where information goes to die. Lead with the alert, end with the question.

Less is more (until it isn't)

Too few lines and the model hallucinates connections. Too many and it misses the real ones. The right number depends on signal density, not volume.

Structure like a briefing, not a dump

Situation first, evidence next, question last. This isn't just good UX — it's aligned with how causal attention actually works.

The Discipline

"Finding the smallest possible set of high-signal tokens that maximize the likelihood of a desired outcome."

— Anthropic Engineering, on context engineering

Intelligence isn't the bottleneck. Context is. The discipline breaks down into four operations:

🔍

Selection

Choosing what goes in — and what stays out. The most important decision you make.

↕

Ordering

Placing information where the model attends best. Start and end, not the middle.

📋

Structuring

Formatting so the model can parse efficiently. Briefing, not raw dump.

🎯

Scoping

Right-sizing the context for the task. Every token earns its place.

This applies whether you're summarizing incident logs, assisting a doctor with a differential diagnosis, analyzing a legal contract, or triaging support tickets. The domain changes. The discipline doesn't.

At Saarthi, we apply this across healthcare and incident management. The doctor in an ER and the engineer on-call at 3am have the same need: the right information, structured for fast decisions, delivered before the situation gets worse.

The Takeaway

Next time someone says "just increase the context window" or "just send it all the data":

  • More context often means worse results (Lost in the Middle)
  • Long-context retrieval is unreliable (Needle in the Haystack)
  • Prompt structure changes model behavior (Prompt Repetition)

The LLM is the reasoning engine. Context engineering is everything else — and it's where the real work happens.
