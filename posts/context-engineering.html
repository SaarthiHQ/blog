Context Engineering Is Not Context Stuffing

By Ramanan Sivasubramanian · March 2026

About Saarthi

Expert AI systems through technical innovation. End-to-end managed solutions for healthcare, incident management, and more.

github.com/SaarthiHQ

In my previous post, I argued that context engineering is reshaping on-call. A few people asked the obvious question: why can't you just give the LLM all the logs and let it figure it out?

Because that makes things worse, not better.

Two Domains, Same Problem

At Saarthi, we build AI systems for two very different users: doctors in ERs and ICUs, and on-call engineers during production incidents. Different domains. Same bottleneck.

A nephrologist receiving a referral from a cardiologist doesn't need the patient's full history. They need creatinine trends, current medications (especially nephrotoxic ones), and the specific question the cardiologist is asking. A 50-page record is noise. Five relevant data points are signal.

An SRE getting paged about a payment service failure doesn't need every log line from every service. They need error logs from the payment service, logs from its upstream dependencies, and the most recent deploy. 5,000 lines of health checks is noise. The 5 lines showing the cascade is signal.

In both cases, the human is the expert reasoner. The doctor makes the clinical judgment. The engineer makes the triage call. But before they can reason, something needs to assemble the right context.

Why "Just Use AI" Fails

Models have 200K token context windows now, so just send everything? The research says otherwise.

Lost in the Middle

Liu et al. · Stanford · 2023

U-shaped attention curve. With 20+ documents, performance dropped below the no-context baseline. More data made the model less accurate than giving it nothing.

Needle in the Haystack

Nelson et al. · IBM Research · 2024

Even simple fact retrieval — finding a single planted sentence — breaks down as context length increases.

Prompt Repetition

Leviathan et al. · Google Research · 2025

The order of information materially changes output. Repeating the query: 47/70 accuracy wins, 0 regressions.

CodeRabbit's study of 470 GitHub PRs (2026): AI models "infer patterns statistically, not semantically." They miss business logic and context that practitioners internalize.

"Input quality directly correlates with output reliability."

A model summarizing 5,000 raw log lines will produce a confident summary that misses the root cause — because the signal was buried at position 2,500 where the model's attention is weakest.

The Abstention Problem

AbstentionBench (June 2025) evaluated 20 frontier models on their ability to say "I don't know."

Scaling doesn't help abstention. Reasoning makes it worse.

Reasoning-trained models showed a 24% drop in abstention vs their non-reasoning counterparts. Teaching a model to reason harder makes it less likely to admit it doesn't know. The path to expert AI is not "make the model smarter."

What Context Engineering Actually Is

Anthropic defines it as finding "the smallest possible set of high-signal tokens that maximize the likelihood of a desired outcome." An engineering discipline, not a prompt trick.

🔍

Selecting what matters

Knowing which sources to query based on the question and domain structure. An expert knows what to look at and what to skip.

🗜

Compressing noise

The same error appears 47 times. Deduplication and pattern normalization reduce thousands of lines to hundreds without losing signal.

🎯

Scoring by relevance

Different question types need different evidence prioritized. Not all evidence is equally useful.

⚠

Assessing quality

Knowing what you don't have. If a source is unreachable, that's information. The model needs to see its own blind spots.

📋

Structuring for attention

Arranging context so the model attends to what matters. Aligned with how transformer attention works.

The Assembly-Reasoning Split

Working across healthcare and incident management led us to a realization: expertise has two components, often performed by different actors.

Two Components of Expertise

Intelligent Assembly

Knowing what to gather, for whom, when

Pattern matching on what-context-helps-when

Domain compression, self-knowledge, persistence

3/5 properties
+

Domain Reasoning

Drawing conclusions from assembled context

Weighing hypotheses, deciding actions

Ambiguity judgment, principled refusal

2/5 properties

In most high-stakes domains, the human is the reasoner. The system's expertise is in assembly.

The higher-value problem

Not "how do we make AI reason better" — but "how do we make AI assemble context like an expert would." Assembly is where 60% of the expert value lives. Reasoning is an optional downstream consumer.

The Uncomfortable Implication

With disciplined context engineering, a smaller model can approach the output quality of a larger model on structured tasks. The ACE framework (ICLR 2026) demonstrated this. At the ARC Prize 2025, a 76K-parameter system outperformed models 1,000x its size through structured representation.

If context engineering is good enough, the model tier becomes a cost lever, not a quality lever.

What This Doesn't Solve

×

The data doesn't exist. No amount of engineering conjures data that was never logged.

×

Novel reasoning needed. A cascading failure with a non-obvious trigger needs human judgment.

×

Inherently ambiguous. When signals contradict, a human needs to resolve it.

The goal is to make expert-level decisions faster — not to eliminate the expert.

Where This Goes

The teams that get this right will have measurably better outcomes — not because they use a fancier model, but because they engineer the context that feeds it. The domain changes. The discipline doesn't.
